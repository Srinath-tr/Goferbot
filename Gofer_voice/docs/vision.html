A Brief History Of Conversational AI

The Turing Test was proposed by legendary computer science pioneer Alan Turing as a way of measuring the intelligence of a computer program. Basically, the test challenges an artifical intelligence program to convince a human judge that it is human. The Turing Test was fiirst proposed in 1950, and despite a half-century of progress in the field of Artificial Intelligence, not a single program has ever passed it.

Nevertheless, people keep trying. Every year, a handful of the world's best conversational AI programs square off in the Loebner Prize Content (basically a formalized Turing Test, with a grand prize of US$100,000 to the first program to fool the human judges). However, after looking at the results of the 2003 Loebner Prize Contest, it's clear that conversational AI programs have a long way to go before they have any hope of passing as humans.

Several of the "best" Loebner entries are available on the Web. You can test your conversational mettle against MegaHAL, Eliza, or A.L.I.C.E. (the closest thing Howie has to a mother).

Proud To Flunk The Turing Test!

After conversing with a few chatterbots, or reading over conversational transcripts, one begins to notice a pattern: computers really aren't that interesting to talk to. Most conversations start out innocently enough, exchanging pleasantries and such. Before too long, however, the conversation devolves into one of the following scenarios:

  • The human begins propositioning the computer for sex. This behavior is so universally predictable that most chatterbots are actually quite comfortable with the topic. (Try this with A.L.I.C.E., incidentally).
  • The computer suffers a logic error, and spits out a non sequitor. Depending on how interesting/humorous the computer's response is, the human continues to feed in increasingly nonsensical input, curious to see how the program will respond.
  • The program suffers from severe short-term memory loss (an affliction which is sadly widespread in modern conversational AIs), and becomes unable to remember anything further back than the most recent human statement. The conversation quickly becomes a stream of content-free conversational no-ops (e.g. "Ah, I understand.", "You do?", "Yes.", "Good.", "You are pleased?", "Yes.", "Excellent.", "Thank you.", "You're welcome!", "Don't mention it.", and so on). Eventually, the human loses interest.

In short, you aren't going to see Howie entering the Loebner Prize Contest any time soon. Howie is proud to stand and be counted among the losers!
